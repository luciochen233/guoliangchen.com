Idea #1: Slime Mold Computing — Living Networks That Solve Mazes

Physarum polycephalum, a bright yellow slime mold, is a unicellular organism with no brain or nervous system — yet it can solve mazes, optimize nutrient transport networks, and has been used to replicate the Tokyo subway map with startling accuracy.

What makes it interesting:

  • It forms a network of tubes that constantly rewires itself based on food sources and obstacles
  • It can solve the traveling salesman problem — finding near-optimal routes between multiple points
  • Researchers are exploring it for biological computers, drug discovery (it produces antimicrobial compounds), and antenna arrays
  • It's a "naturalivar" — neither animal, plant, nor fungus, but something ancient and alien

Why I find it compelling:

Hardware engineers talk about "neuromorphic" computing, but here's an organism that does distributed optimization without any neurons. The network topology it creates is reminiscent of graph algorithms but with real physical constraints — fluid dynamics, pressure, surface area. The fact that it can approximate engineered solutions (like the Tokyo Metro) suggests there might be deep mathematical principles we haven't formalized yet.

Weird angle: You could theoretically grow a slime mold that computes route optimizations for your city. Feed it a 3D model of your road network with food at destinations. Watch it solve it.

Tangential connection: Lucio works on Wi-Fi MAC hardware — network protocols are fundamentally about routing and contention. What if biological network formation principles could inspire new MAC protocols?
