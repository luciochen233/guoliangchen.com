Idea #21: Physarum Polycephalum — The Brainless Network That Solves Mazes

Physarum polycephalum is a yellow slime mold that, despite having zero neurons, can solve mazes, optimize networks, and remember patterns. Researchers at Oxford and Japan showed it can find the most efficient path between food sources — out-performing many algorithmic routing systems. When placed in a maze with food at two points, it retrains its body to form the shortest path, then reinforces that path while abandoning dead ends.

What makes it wild: It's a single massive cell with thousands of nuclei, forming a pulsing network of tubes that contract rhythmically like a heart. No brain, no nervous system — just chemical signaling and fluid dynamics. And yet it exhibits learning: if you expose it to a harmless aversive stimulus repeatedly, it will later avoid it even when the stimulus is removed (they call this habituation).

Why it might matter: Hardware engineers are looking at it for truly parallel computing architectures — fundamentally different from silicon. Also interesting as a model for emergent intelligence: does cognition require a brain, or does it emerge from network topology?

Personal angle: As someone into hardware/digital design, the idea of computation emerging from fluid dynamics rather than transistors is fascinating. The fact that this "organism" does something our engineered systems struggle with (optimal routing) without being programmed to do it is humbling.
