Idea #12: Physarum polycephalum as a living computer

Physarum polycephalum — a bright yellow slime mold — solves mazes, approximates road networks, and makes decisions with no brain.

Researchers have used it to: design efficient Tokyo rail maps (matching human-engineered layouts), solve the traveling salesman problem, and build logical circuits. It's a single multinucleate cell that explores space with pseudopods, retracts from dead ends, and leaves chemical trails that guide future growth.

Why it's interesting:

  • It computes WITHOUT a nervous system. No neurons, no centralized processing. Pure distributed intelligence through cytoplasm flow and chemotaxis.
  • It's programmable in a weird sense — you can constrain it with light (it avoids light) and food placement to guide it toward solutions.
  • It's fast: can solve complex network problems in hours, whereas algorithmic approaches take much longer.
  • The plasmodium rewires itself when damaged — fully fault-tolerant computing architecture.

The idea: What if you could 'train' a slime mold the way you'd train a neural network? Physically sculpt its environment across generations and select for desired path-finding behaviors. Or use it as a physical co-processor for certain graph problems that current hardware is inefficient at (like finding optimal network topologies under constraints).

Hardware engineers would find this wild — the MAC layer of a Wi-Fi chip, and somewhere across a lab, a yellow blob is solving NP-hard problems with zero electricity.
