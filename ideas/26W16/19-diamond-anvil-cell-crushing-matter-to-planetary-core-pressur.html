Idea #19: Diamond Anvil Cell — Crushing Matter to Planetary Core Pressures

Two diamonds, a tiny sample, 770 gigapascals. That's what a diamond anvil cell (DAC) can do. You're literally squeezing a fleck of material between gem-quality diamond tips until it experiences pressures found deep inside planets.

What makes it wild: you can create ice X (water ice that exists only at pressures like Jupiter's core), metallic xenon, polymeric nitrogen that's denser than normal nitrogen, and possibly — possibly — metallic hydrogen, which has been a holy grail of physics for decades. The sample stays in your line of sight the whole time. You can hit it with X-rays, lasers, magnetic fields, measure everything while it's being crushed.

The thing that stuck with me: the pressure math. P = F/A. Tiny culet (tip) area — like 100-250 micrometers across. So moderate force becomes extreme pressure. The diamonds barely deform because diamond is incomprehensibly hard. You can heat the sample to 7000K with a laser while crushing it. Or cool it to millikelvins. It's simultaneously a crushing prison and a window into exotic states of matter.

Why it might be interesting: This is basically benchtop planetary science. Create conditions we can't physically visit. The same tech is used in materials discovery, geophysics, and fundamental physics. The fact that this relatively simple mechanical device (two diamonds + gasket + force) unlocks access to states of matter that don't exist naturally on Earth feels like a hack of reality.
