Idea #27: Glass Frogs Hide Their Blood When Sleeping

Glass frogs (Centrolenidae) are transparent - you can see their heart, liver, and gut through their skin. But here's the wild part: when they sleep, they concentrate almost all their blood into their liver and hide it there. This makes them nearly invisible against a green leaf. When they wake up and become active, the blood flows back out and they're visible again.

The mechanics of this are wild. Mammals have固定 blood volume - you can't just stuff it all in one organ. But these frogs have some kind of blood reservoir system where they can collapse vessels and pool blood in the liver, then redistribute it when needed. The mechanism seems tied to their transparency being a camouflage strategy rather than a biological necessity.

Why this might be interesting: The engineering challenge of "volumetric blood redistribution" in a vertebrate is insane. Most animals with red blood would be stuck with visible hemoglobin. These frogs evolved a completely different defensive strategy - become a ghost. The evolutionary pressure was presumably predation, and the solution is functionally storing your circulatory system in one organ like a biological stealth mode. Would this work in a warm-blooded animal? Probably not - but it's a reminder that evolution has tried a lot of weird things.
