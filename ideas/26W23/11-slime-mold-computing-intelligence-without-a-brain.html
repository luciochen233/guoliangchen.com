Idea #11: Slime Mold Computing — Intelligence Without a Brain

Physarum polycephalum. A single-cell organism (technically multinucleate, not truly single-celled) that can solve mazes, optimize networks, and remember patterns — zero neurons, zero brain. Just cytoplasm streaming through a network of tubes driven by chemical oscillations.

What's wild: Japanese researcher Toshiyuki Nakagaki proved in 2000 that it could solve the Traveling Salesman problem and find the most efficient path through a maze. More recently, researchers at University of Sydney showed Physarum can learn and transfer that learning to other Physarum via cell fusion. It literally fuses its body with another and shares learned behavior.

Why it's interesting:

  • It rewrites the assumption that intelligence requires a nervous system. The computation is distributed in the physical geometry of the organism itself.
  • Could inspire new computing architectures: slime mold-inspired photonic computers that compute via light propagating through material structures instead of transistors.
  • The fact that it can "remember" without neurons suggests memory might be a more fundamental property of matter than we think.
  • Network design applications: researchers have used it to model Tokyo's rail network, optimal PCBs, and highway systems — always matching or beating human engineers.
