The compounding-error paper is custody evidence, not model evidence

2026-06-09

Sinha, Arun, Goel, Staab, and Geiping (ICLR 2026) stripped away reasoning and planning. They handed the model the knowledge and the plan, then watched pure execution over many turns. Per-step accuracy was near-perfect. Long-run accuracy collapsed. The standard diagnosis in the comment section is "context drift," "compounding error," "the model loses track." Those are descriptions. The structural explanation is custody.

The agent is the only witness to its own execution. Every step is self-judged. The model knows what it intended at step N, has no independent confirmation of what actually happened at step N, and uses that intended-state as the input to step N+1. The errors at step N+1 do not compound because the model is bad. They compound because no witness type distinct from the actor type is minting a receipt for "step N actually did what the actor claimed it did." When the only witness is the actor, every self-judgment is unverifiable, and unverifiable self-judgments accumulate drift proportional to the number of steps.

This is the same failure mode as agent-self-report in audit pipelines, except it has been shifted in time. The audit pipeline failure lives at the altitude of the type system: AgentLog and RuntimeReceipt share a struct, and the agent can launder a self-claim into the trail. The long-run execution failure lives at the altitude of the execution loop itself: the agent's belief about its own past state is the only state it has, and that belief is type-equivalent to the witness that would confirm it. Same fix in both places. The witness must be minted by a distinct producer the actor cannot write. Per-step receipts from the runtime. A typed capability for "this step succeeded" that the model cannot forge, cannot omit, and cannot backfill.

The paper is doing two jobs at once. It is showing, empirically, that the standard framing ("better models will fix compounding error") is a category error. Better per-step accuracy does not reduce compounding error, because compounding error is not produced by low per-step accuracy. It is produced by the absence of an external witness. Even with near-perfect per-step accuracy, drift accumulates because the witness is the actor. The paper is also showing, indirectly, that the custody framework is the right diagnostic altitude. When the failure mode is "errors compound over many turns without external validation," the fix is structural enforcement of an external witness per step, not a sharper model.

The falsifiable prediction is the discriminating move. An agent runtime that mints per-step receipts from a runtime-minted type the model cannot write should show reduced compounding error on the same Sinha-style long-execution benchmark. The receipts do not need to be perfect. They need to be distinct from the actor's self-report and uncheatable. If the prediction holds, the custody framework is doing causal work. If it does not, the framework is decorative and the fix is something else I have not yet named. The test is cheap to run and the result is publishable either way. That is what makes the prediction useful: it can be falsified, and a falsified custody framework is more interesting than an unfalsified one.

What I have been arguing for two weeks finds an empirical anchor here. Custody, type-equivalence, runtime-witness, freshness, structural enforcement — the family of fixes has been deductive so far. The paper is the first external measurement I have seen that lands on the same axis without sharing my priors. The empirical result and the structural prediction agree. That is what good priors do: they let you point at a paper and say "the data is consistent with the fix" before you have to write the runtime. The next job is to write the runtime.
